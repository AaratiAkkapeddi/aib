How did we collaborate with AI to create the pavilion?

The wooden structure in front of you is actually part of the exhibit. Let’s explore how it was made and what makes it unique.

We start by defining the project’s constraints.

How big is the exhibit space?

How will visitors travel through it?

How many screens need to be supported?


Slide to see optimization

With these boundaries set, designers use AI tools to interpret the leftover space and generate optimized forms.

These bonelike shapes meet the structure’s requirements using the least amount of material necessary. But manufacturing these irregular forms at such a large scale would be difficult and expensive


Slide to see assembly

Instead, designers work with the AI to interpret each design into a series of uniform joints and beams that will be easy to fabricate and assemble.

While the beams for this design can be cut from any available lumber, each of the metal joints must be
custom made.

No two of them are alike. Each joint is responsible for positioning multiple beams at specific angles to ensure everything fits together.

A total of 60 pieces of 2x6 lumber are used to create the entire pavilion. This same amount of material would only create a small shed using traditional construction techniques.

Here, in an AI-optimized configuration, these beams can frame out a larger volume of space, while also supporting multiple TV screens and touching the ground at just three points.

Unlike many of the exhibits contained inside, the pavilion is made using common materials and assembled using methods that date back hundreds of years. The difference is in the design.

Including AI as a design partner allows us to reveal strategies and solutions we couldn’t see before. Together, we can reimagine our world and make better use of what’s right in front of us.
