How can we search for life on distant planets?


The search for life in deep space is a job for robots. Making these robots is a job for JPL, NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory.

Their challenge is to design an interplanetary lander packed with the maximum possible amount of sensors and test equipment to make the trip worthwhile.

When it comes to launching things into space, weight is key. Every 2 pounds of the lander’s weight requires nearly 1 ton of fuel and engine mass from the rocket that carries it.

Every ounce matters, but in this case an ounce of scientific cargo is more valuable than the lander that delivers it. The lighter the vessel, the more tools can be packed for exploration.

For this lander prototype, the JPL team turned to AI as a design partner. By feeding in design parameters, engineers can use AI to explore large sets of possible solutions. After a period of trial and error, the strongest designs are refined and tested.

To qualify all of the structures for spaceflight, every component is made from aluminum, a lightweight metal that has proven its durability in space.

After simulating how the AI-optimized designs would benefit from different manufacturing techniques the team decides to use a mix of fabrication methods, each suited for distinct sections of the lander.
Milling
Casting
3d Printing
Each manufacturing technique offers unique advantages, allowing parts to be further optimized for weight, strength, or complexity. In each case, AI tools are able to adapt the design to make the most of how it’s fabricated.
Sand casting is used to create the lander’s chassis, the largest element of the design.

Casting is an ideal way to create this large, hollow frame. Because everything attaches to this central structure, it’s covered with strategically placed holes for mounting the legs and other components.

The skeleton-like appearance is a result of AI optimization. Borrowing from the same concept of natural selection that guides a bone’s shape over generations, the AI calculates its design in an accelerated process of trial and error. Here, it leaves behind only what’s essential for the requirements of the frame.
To maximize strength, the lander’s legs are carved from solid blocks of aluminum using a computer controlled mill.
The AI informs every aspect of the process–from which types of milling bits to use, to the sequence of paths they should take across the aluminum.
The most intricate, internal elements of the lander’s design are made using an industrial 3D printer. The process builds the design up layer by layer using aluminum powder, selectively fusing each layer to the last.

Fully assembled, the concept design for this interplanetary lander achieved a weight reduction of 35% compared to JPL’s baseline standards.

Because the body is custom fit for the payload, no space is wasted. Leaving more room for sensors and scientific instruments. Precious cargo that will act as our eyes and ears on distant planets.
It’s a breakthrough made possible by partnering with AI through every stage of the process. By pushing the limits of aerospace design, we can open new approaches to the search for life beyond our planet.
