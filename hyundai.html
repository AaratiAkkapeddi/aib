How can we combine
a car and a robot?


There are limits to where even the toughest off-road vehicles can go.

Places with steep or uneven terrain where no conventional car could reach.
How do you make a vehicle that can move through places that are only accessible by foot?

The answer may be a car with legs.

Massive legs, with wheels for feet, and a design that borrows more from robotics than automobiles.

As humans we take walking for granted.

From an engineering perspective, it’s a delicate coordination of joints, muscles, and reflexes that’s difficult to replicate mechanically.

By coordinating four of these unique legs, this concept vehicle from Hyundai can not only roll like a car and walk like a horse, but also crawl low to the ground like a reptile.

Mammalian ModeReptilian Mode
Walking in mammalian mode provides additional height for clearing obstacles and a greater range of vertical leg motion, useful for climbing.
By contrast, the wide gait of the reptilian walking
mode provides increased stability and a lower
center of gravity, ideal for uneven terrain.
Without muscles, the movements of each leg depend on heavy, high-torque electric motors in each joint. Striking the right balance requires subtracting mass from the supports. Just like bones, these supports must be both strong and light.
To reduce the weight of the leg while maximizing its strength, designers used AI-powered design tools to create, test, and evolve hundreds of potential designs in minutes.

Michell Truss

In this case, the software generated these curved lattices of material following a mathematical algorithm called a Michell Truss.
From these generated solutions, designers consider the
trade-offs of strength, weight, cost, manufacturing

complexity and sustainability.

A handful of options are selected and then prototyped using a mix of experimental materials and processes.

Slide to expand model

After a series of revisions, the team arrives at the design you see here.

The structure uses a minimum of material, organized to provide support where it’s needed most.

In many places, magnesium casting is used for the final pieces, offering the best balance of strength and weight.

For smaller payloads, Hyundai designed a scaled-down version of the vehicle.

Its smaller footprint and lighter weight make it possible to be deployed by drone.

Once dropped off, onboard computers allow it to drive itself to remote locations.

The transforming robots of childhood fantasy are now made possible by human ingenuity, aided by AI.

Now that you’ve seen how automobile technology and robotics can combine into an exciting new breed of vehicle, ask yourself: how might we adapt these same breakthroughs to other forms of transportation?
