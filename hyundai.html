Current Story: How can we combine a car and a robot?

Today’s off-road vehicles are a great choice for adventurous drivers taking on rough terrain. But there are still limits to where even the toughest vehicles can go. Places untouched by automobiles because of a steep or uneven landscape.

Hyundai sees this limitation as an opportunity to create a new breed of vehicle capable of navigating extreme terrain.
How do you make a car that can drive places previously only accessible by foot? The straightforward approach is to make a car that can walk.

As humans we take walking for granted, but it’s a challenging balancing act for engineers to recreate in robotics.

Fortunately, Hyundai’s experience in both automotive manufacturing and advanced robotics makes them uniquely suited to the challenge of creating a walking car.

From your hip to your toes—how many joints are in your leg?

How many directions can each joint turn?

In the case of Hyundai’s Elevate concept vehicle, high-torque electric motors are used for each joint within a leg. Joints that not only extend, but also twist.



Each of these motors adds more weight that the leg must support, along with the total weight of the vehicle. For the concept to succeed, weight must be removed from the supports between the motors. These act as the bones of the leg. And just like your bones, they must be both strong and light.

In the case of Hyundai’s Elevate concept vehicle, high-torque electric motors are used for each joint within a leg. Joints that not only extend, but also twist.

Each of these motors adds more weight that the leg must support, along with the total weight of the vehicle. For the concept to succeed, weight must be removed from the supports between the motors. These act as the bones of the leg. And just like your bones, they must be both strong and light.

Nature perfects its formula for bones using trial and error over thousands of years until it finds the perfect balance of shape and strength.

By harnessing Autodesk’s generative design tools, Hyundai can test and evolve thousands of potential leg designs, working within the constraints defined by the project team.

From these generated solutions, Hyundai’s designers use their judgement to pick a handful worthy of further consideration.

Trade-offs of strength, weight, cost, manufacturing complexity and sustainability clearly illustrated early in the process.

Futuristic materials such as metallic foams, carbon fiber and new metal alloys, are explored as methods for creating components that would be unimaginable with traditional methods.

After a series of prototypes, tests, and revisions, the team arrived at the design you see here. The structure uses a minimum of material, organized to support strain where it’s needed most.

To remove as much weight as possible, normally solid sections are often replaced with honeycomb-like lattices of material.

The patterns may look haphazard, but each structure is designed to maximize strength, responding to the simulated stresses of motor torque and payload compression.

Finding the right design isn’t just a question of shape. Materials and manufacturing methods are just as crucial. In many places, magnesium casting is used to create the final pieces, offering the best strength to weight ratio.

The result is a vehicle that can not only roll like a car and walk like a mammal, but also crawl low to the ground like a reptile for added stability.

For smaller, unmanned payloads, Hyundai designed a scaled-down version of the Elevate nicknamed Tiger.

Perfect for delivering supplies to remote locations, Tiger’s smaller footprint and lighter weight make it possible to be delivered by drone.

Once it’s dropped off, onboard computers allow Tiger to drive itself to its destination, all while keeping its payload level.

These transforming robots of childhood fantasy are now made possible by human ingenuity, aided by AI.

Now that you’ve seen how automobile technology and robotics can combine into an exciting new breed of vehicle, how might we adapt these same breakthroughs to other forms of transportation?
